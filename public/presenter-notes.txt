GENERAL TIPS
------------
- Audience: Technical reviewers (data team, analytics leads, possibly IT/Finance)
- Tone: Precise, data-driven, no hand-waving. This audience will push back
  if numbers don't add up or methodology is glossed over.
- Pacing: ~3-4 minutes per slide, ~50-60 minutes total for 22 slides

SLIDE 2 — EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
================================================================================
GOAL: Land the business problem immediately before going into any technical detail.

KEY IDEAS TO CONVEY:
- "Allianz currently captures only 21% direct debit adoption. The market
  average is 65%. That is a 44-percentage-point operational gap."
- "Direct debit is not a preference feature — it directly improves cash-flow
  predictability, reduces collection costs, and shortens the receivables cycle."
- "We built a scoring engine that ranks all non-DD contracts by their predicted

  following slides — this is just the bottom line."

ANTICIPATED QUESTION: "How do you know it is 65% market average?"
ANSWER: This is the industry benchmark figure cited in the project brief.
It is the business sponsor's baseline, not a figure the model produced.

GOAL: Brief transition. Use it to set context before the data slides.

SLIDE 4 — THE DATASET AT SCALE
================================================================================
GOAL: Establish dataset credibility and data quality process.

KEY IDEAS TO CONVEY:
- "452,000+ rows is production scale. This is not a small academic dataset."
- "Each row is a contract — one customer can appear multiple times across
  different products. This is important because we are not modelling customers,
  we are modelling contracts."
- "Data quality was strong: less than 0.001% missing values. 64 duplicate rows
  were removed."
- "Two data quality interventions worth noting:
    1. Broker_cor had outlier values as extreme as 6 × 10^16 — clearly an
       extraction error. We capped at the 95th percentile as a pragmatic fix.
    2. Annual_premium is heavily right-skewed (max ~3M EUR vs. mean ~513 EUR).
       We applied a log-transform to stabilise the distribution for the models."
- "ID columns — contract number, customer ID, broker ID — were excluded from
  features. Including them would cause memorisation, not generalisation."

================================================================================
SLIDE 5 — CLASS IMBALANCE
================================================================================
GOAL: Explain why this is a hard classification problem and how we addressed it.

KEY IDEAS TO CONVEY:
- "The target variable is imbalanced: 79% of contracts do NOT have direct debit,
  21% do. That is a roughly 3.76:1 imbalance."
- "The naive baseline — a model that always predicts 'no direct debit' —
  would achieve 79% accuracy without learning anything useful. Accuracy is
  therefore the wrong metric here."
- "We handled imbalance in two ways:
    1. class_weight='balanced' for all scikit-learn classifiers — this
       automatically adjusts loss weights inversely proportional to class frequency.
    2. scale_pos_weight=3.76 for XGBoost — the native parameter that serves
       the same purpose."
- "Our primary evaluation metrics were ROC-AUC, F1-Score, and Recall.
  These are robust to class imbalance. Accuracy is reported only for completeness."

ANTICIPATED QUESTION: "Why not use SMOTE or oversampling?"
ANSWER: "With 360K+ training rows, resampling introduces memory overhead and
risk of data leakage if applied before the train/test split. Class weighting
achieves the same correction without those risks, and it is more efficient
at this scale."

================================================================================
SLIDE 6 — EDA KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================
GOAL: Preview the two most important signals — payment frequency dominance
and the leakage risk — before the modelling section.

KEY IDEAS TO CONVEY:
- "The EDA revealed six patterns worth highlighting for the modelling decisions."
- "Payment frequency is the single dominant signal. Monthly contracts are
  nearly universally on direct debit — but this is because Allianz mandates it
  for monthly payers. This is a business rule masquerading as a predictive feature.
  We will address this explicitly in the leakage analysis slide."
- "Customer segment, type, geography, and line of business all show meaningful
  variation in DD adoption rates — these are the real behavioural signals
  we want the model to learn."
- "Note that Annual_premium was log-transformed precisely because of the
  extreme skew we saw in EDA."

SLIDE 7 — SECTION BREAK: MODELLING
================================================================================
GOAL: Transition to the technical core of the presentation.

SAY: "Now let's walk through the pipeline architecture and the five models
we trained. I want to give the technical rationale for each one — why we
included it and what its trade-offs are."

================================================================================
SLIDE 8 — FEATURE ENGINEERING & PIPELINE
================================================================================
GOAL: Show the pipeline is clean, reproducible, and deployment-ready.

KEY IDEAS TO CONVEY:
- "We created three engineered features:
    - Premium_log: the log-transform of annual premium.
    - Is_monthly_payment: a binary flag to make the monthly payment signal
      explicit for tree-based models.
    - Broker_profitable: a binary flag when the broker's combined ratio
      is below 100% — a proxy for broker quality."
- "The preprocessing pipeline uses scikit-learn's ColumnTransformer with two
  branches: categorical (impute → one-hot encode) and numerical (impute → scale)."
- "Critically, all preprocessing is inside the pipeline object. There is no
  data leakage from fit-transforming on the full dataset — the preprocessor
  is fit only on the training set."
- "The train/test split was 80/20 with stratification to preserve class ratios:
  361,725 training rows and 90,432 test rows."

ANTICIPATED QUESTION: "Why not use target encoding for high-cardinality categoricals?"
ANSWER: "One-hot encoding was chosen for interpretability and compatibility
across all five models. Target encoding was considered but introduces leakage
risk if not handled carefully in cross-validation. With the cardinalities
present in this dataset, OHE was tractable."

================================================================================
SLIDE 9 — FIVE CLASSIFIERS
================================================================================
GOAL: Justify the model selection strategy to a technical audience.

KEY IDEAS TO CONVEY:
- "We deliberately chose models from different families to get a diagnostic
  picture of the problem structure."
- "Decision Tree: interpretability baseline. The learned decision rules can be
  shared with brokers and business teams as plain English logic."
- "Random Forest: the ensemble upgrade from the Decision Tree. Reduces
  variance through 200-tree averaging and provides feature importances."
- "Logistic Regression: the gold-standard linear baseline. Coefficients are
  directly interpretable as odds ratios, making it easy to explain to Finance."
- "SVM: computationally infeasible on 360K+ rows at full scale, so we trained
  on a stratified 50K sample. Included for theoretical completeness."
- "XGBoost: the expected winner on tabular data. Gradient boosting corrects
  residuals iteratively and handles sparse OHE features efficiently.
  scale_pos_weight handles the class imbalance natively."

SLIDE 10 — SECTION BREAK: EVALUATION
================================================================================
GOAL: Brief pause before the numbers. Remind audience of the metrics framework.

SAY: "We now have results from all five models on the held-out 90K test set.
Before I show the table, let me remind you: we are optimising for ROC-AUC
as the primary metric, followed by F1-Score. Accuracy is shown but is not
the decision criterion."

SLIDE 11 — MODEL COMPARISON TABLE
================================================================================
GOAL: Present results clearly and explain the selection logic.

KEY IDEAS TO CONVEY:
- "All five models cluster at ~98.5% accuracy and ~96.3% F1. This tight
  clustering is expected — and actually a sign that the problem has strong
  structural signal."
- "On ROC-AUC, XGBoost leads at 97.87%, followed by Random Forest at 97.73%.
  The gap is modest but consistent."
- "The selection decision: XGBoost is recommended as the production scoring model
  because it has the highest ROC-AUC and is the most appropriate architecture
  for tabular, sparse-encoded data at this scale."
- "Decision Tree remains valuable as the explainability companion —
  its rules can be translated into plain business logic for broker training,
  even though its AUC is slightly lower."
- "SVM, trained on only 50K rows, cannot be fairly compared at full scale.
  It is included for reference, not production."

ANTICIPATED QUESTION: "Why not just pick Random Forest? Its F1 is marginally
better than XGBoost."
ANSWER: "F1 differences at the fourth decimal place are not operationally
significant. ROC-AUC is the decisive tie-breaker because it reflects the
quality of probability calibration across the full range of thresholds.
XGBoost leads on this metric."

================================================================================
SLIDE 12 — DATA LEAKAGE DISCOVERY
================================================================================
GOAL: This is one of the most technically important slides. Show that the team
identified, isolated, and quantified the leakage — and that it was handled
responsibly.

KEY IDEAS TO CONVEY:
- "Payment_frequency_Monthly appears as the dominant feature in importance
  rankings. This seems like a great predictor — until you understand why."
- "Allianz's business rule: monthly payment contracts MUST use direct debit.
  This is not a model learning a pattern — it is the model memorising a rule
  that already exists in the data. This is textbook data leakage."
- "To quantify the leakage, we ran Strategy B: a parallel model with
  Payment_frequency and Is_monthly_payment excluded entirely."
- "The result: ROC-AUC dropped from 97.87% to 75.56%. That 22-point drop
  is the 'free lunch' the business rule was handing the model."
- "75.56% AUC is still meaningfully above random (50%) — this is the genuine
  signal from customer demographics, geography, segment, and product features."
- "Strategy A is still valid for operational scoring of the full portfolio,
  because payment frequency IS known for existing contracts. Strategy B is
  the right model for understanding voluntary adoption drivers."

ANTICIPATED QUESTION: "Does this invalidate Strategy A?"
ANSWER: "No. For the scoring use case — prioritising which existing non-DD
contracts to target — we know the payment frequency already. Strategy A's
high AUC is operationally valid for that task. Strategy B gives us insight
into the non-frequency drivers, which is useful for campaign messaging."

SLIDE 15 — PORTFOLIO SCORING
================================================================================
GOAL: Show the operational output and quantify the lift.

KEY IDEAS TO CONVEY:

- "The top 5,000 contracts have a predicted conversion probability of 24.97%."

SLIDE 16 — CONTROLLED PILOT
================================================================================
GOAL: Explain why a controlled pilot is the right first step before full rollout.

KEY IDEAS TO CONVEY:
- "We recommend a 90-day A/B pilot before committing to full portfolio rollout."
- "Treatment group: top 5,000 contracts by model score. They receive targeted
  outreach (email, broker call, app notification)."
- "Control group: 5,000 randomly selected non-DD contracts. They receive
  nothing."
- "After 90 days, we compare actual conversion rates between the two groups.
  The delta is the true attribution of the model."
- "Why is this important? Because our model predicts propensity — not causal
  uplift. Some high-probability contracts might have converted anyway.
  The pilot separates propensity from incremental lift."
- "The pilot output also allows us to calibrate the decision threshold.
  Is 50% the right cut-off? Or 30%? The response data will tell us."
- "The pilot data becomes the training set for a Phase 2 uplift model."

ANTICIPATED QUESTION: "What conversion rate are you expecting?"
ANSWER: "We are not committing to a point estimate before the pilot —
that would be forecasting, not science. The model predicts relative ranking.
The pilot will give us the absolute conversion rates we need for ROI calculation."

================================================================================
GOAL: Translate model insights into actionable campaign differentiation.

KEY IDEAS TO CONVEY:
- "One campaign for all customers is inefficient. The EDA and feature
  importance tell us the messaging and channel must differ by segment."
- "Retail individuals (non-monthly): most natural conversion target.
  Digital outreach works. Emphasise convenience and automatic on-time payments."
- "SME and Mid-Corp: driven by finance/AP teams. Approach via account
  managers and brokers. Messaging should emphasise admin efficiency and

- "Quarterly payers: already accept periodic automatic payment. At renewal,
  a single-click upgrade to direct debit is a very low-friction ask."
- "On broker engagement: the model can generate a per-broker DD conversion
  scorecard. Brokers who see they are below market benchmark become
  motivated advocates for the campaign."

SLIDE 19 — KNOWN LIMITATIONS
================================================================================
GOAL: Present limitations clearly and show each has been thought through.

KEY IDEAS TO CONVEY:
- "Limitation 1 — Payment frequency leakage: Already discussed. Strategy A
  is valid for its use case. Strategy B is preserved for voluntary adoption insight."
- "Limitation 2 — Static snapshot: This dataset represents one moment in time.
  The model will degrade as the portfolio evolves. Quarterly retraining is
  budgeted into the next steps."
- "Limitation 3 — No response history: We predict propensity, not causal lift.
  The pilot solves this by generating labelled response data."
- "Limitation 4 — Broker_cor data quality: The extreme outlier values (up to
  6 × 10^16) indicate a data extraction or encoding error upstream.
  Our P95 cap is pragmatic. The data engineering team should investigate
  the root cause in the source system."
- "Limitation 5 — SVM scalability: The SVM was trained on 50K rows due to
  computational constraints. Its evaluation is informative, not definitive,
  at full portfolio scale."

TONE: State these confidently. Identifying limitations proactively
builds more credibility than omitting them.

SLIDE 20 — ROADMAP TO PRODUCTION
================================================================================
GOAL: Show there is a clear, prioritised path from current state to full deployment.

KEY IDEAS TO CONVEY:
- "Step 1: SHAP analysis. This gives us individual-level explanations —
  'this contract scores high because of X and Y' — which is essential
  for broker conversations and for regulatory auditability."
- "Step 2: Uplift modelling. After the pilot, we fit a causal model on
  the A/B outcomes. This separates propensity from incremental uplift."
- "Step 3: Hyperparameter tuning. GridSearchCV or Optuna on XGBoost.
  Currently using sensible defaults — tuning may yield 1-2 additional
  AUC points."
- "Step 4: API deployment. The joblib pipeline is ready. Wrapping it in
  FastAPI or deploying to Azure ML / AWS SageMaker is a 2-4 week
  engineering task."
- "Step 5: Quarterly retraining schedule. New contract data is generated
  continuously. We propose retraining on a quarterly cadence and
  monitoring for model drift between cycles."
- "The joblib artefact already contains the full preprocessing +
  classification pipeline — no separate inference preprocessing needed."

SLIDE 22 — Q&A
================================================================================
GOAL: Open the floor. Be prepared for the following common technical questions.

LIKELY QUESTIONS FROM TECHNICAL REVIEWERS:

Q: "Did you consider neural networks or deep learning models?"
A: "Neural networks offer marginal gains over XGBoost on tabular data
   at this scale, at significantly higher infrastructure cost and lower
   interpretability. The current ROC-AUC of 97.87% leaves little room
   for meaningful improvement from a more complex architecture."

Q: "Why F1 as a secondary metric rather than Precision-Recall AUC?"
A: "Both are valid. F1 is reported because it is familiar to business
   stakeholders. PR-AUC would give a more granular view of the
   precision-recall trade-off. We can add it to the appendix."

Q: "How do you handle new contracts that have not been seen in training?"
A: "The pipeline uses one-hot encoding with 'Unknown' imputation for
   unseen categories. New contracts are scored in the same pipeline.
   Retraining on fresh data quarterly keeps the model calibrated."

Q: "What is the threshold for the binary decision — 0.5?"
A: "Currently 0.5 as the default. The optimal threshold depends on the
   cost trade-off between false positives (wasted outreach) and false
   negatives (missed conversions). The pilot will generate the data needed
   to optimise this threshold empirically."

Q: "Have you validated against a holdout from a different time period?"
A: "The current evaluation is an 80/20 stratified split from the same
   dataset snapshot. Temporal validation on a more recent contract batch
   is listed as a next step — it would confirm that the model generalises
   across time, not just across contracts in the same snapshot."
